Definition

For most electronic applications, filters are circuit blocks applied to an input signal to achieve a desired output signal by picking out specific components of the signal. Filters are commonly classified as either low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, or band-stop, depending on the type of signals they pick out. In the case of a low pass filter, an input signal is filtered by allowing low-frequency signals to pass without attenuation while rejecting high-frequency signals on the other hand:

A low pass filter can be constructed by using either active components such as transistors and operating amplifiers, or passive components such as resistors and capacitors. Particularly, a first-order passive low pass filter is an electronic circuit block consisting of a single resistor connected in series to a single capacitor. The input voltage is connected in series to the filter, while the output voltage is taken by measuring the voltage drop across the capacitor.

Frequency Response

The output of a passive low-pass filter can be further characterised by observing its frequency response. We can plot the filter's gain , expressed as the ratio between the input and output voltages of the circuit, over the frequency fat which the circuit operates. Since electronic filters are used in a wide range of operating frequencies, it is convenient to make use of logarithmic Bode plots in plotting frequency response curves. Note that the gain of the filter remains constant for lower frequencies, maintaining an output voltage approximately equal to the input voltage. Then at a certain frequency three decibels below the initial value, the gain begins to decrease to a constant slope. This is called the cut-off frequency, corner frequency, or the -3dB frequency of the low-pass filter. At this cut-off frequency, the output voltage is approximately 70.71% of the input voltage. Finally, at higher frequencies beyond the cut-off frequency, the output voltage decreases by a factor of ten (-20dB) for every decade.
